Ideal Client Profile

Who Griggs Electric is actually walking into the home of.

The 12 service-area communities span a real range — from established growth suburbs to some of the wealthiest zip codes in North Texas. That range is the ICP: it's not one household type, it's a ladder, and the brand needs to speak to all of it without alienating either end.

Segment 1 of 3

The Luxury Estate Household

$1.2M – $3M+ homes
Southlake · Westlake · Trophy Club (upper tier) · Bartonville

Westlake is home to Fidelity Investments and a concentration of C-suite executives; Southlake draws steady corporate relocation buyers into Carroll ISD; Vaquero and Entrada estates in Westlake regularly exceed $3M. These are custom estates, golf-course lots, resort-style outdoor living, and smart-home builds — homeowners who have already made it, and whose homes are part of how that shows.

Southlake median home value ≈ $1.65M; Westlake estates commonly $1.6M–$3M+ (Zillow, Redfin, Wynne Moore Group, 2026).
Segment 2 of 3

The Established Affluent Household

$600K – $900K homes
Keller · Grapevine · Argyle · Lantana · Trophy Club (broader)

Comfortable, settled families — often move-up buyers who've been in the area a while, own their home outright or close to it, and treat it as the place they intend to stay. Trophy Club in particular functions as the "executive residential wing" of the Westlake financial corridor — successful, but not flaunting it.

Median home values roughly $600K–$900K across these areas (Zillow, Redfin, 2026).
Segment 3 of 3

The Growing Family Household

$370K – $530K homes
Roanoke · Northlake · Justin · Haslet

Newer construction, growth-corridor communities — families who are building equity and building a life at the same time. Less established wealth, more forward momentum. This is also where Griggs Electric is headquartered, which matters: this is home turf, not just a service radius.

Median home values roughly $370K–$530K across these areas (Zillow, 2026).
The Homes Themselves

What Griggs Electric is being invited into.

Across all three tiers, the actual jobs skew toward homes people are proud of and actively investing in — not emergency repairs on neglected houses.

01

Custom & golf-course estates

Southlake, Westlake, and upper Trophy Club — smart-home wiring, outdoor living and pool electrical, whole-home panel upgrades for renovations.

02

Established family homes mid-remodel

Keller, Grapevine, Argyle, Lantana — kitchen and outdoor remodels, lighting upgrades, the projects that turn a good house into "my husband's favorite room."

03

New-construction and growth-suburb homes

Roanoke, Northlake, Justin, Haslet — new outlets, efficiency upgrades, and the electrical work that comes with a family actively building out their first real home.

What They're Actually Buying

Not electrical work. Peace of mind.

Across every tier, the emotional want is the same — only the stakes and the price tag scale up.

Trust With Their Home

Someone who won't damage the house, track dust through it, or leave a mess behind — treating it the way they'd treat their own.

Zero Worry

Not wanting to think about the electrical again after the crew leaves — no callbacks, no "is that going to be a problem later."

Invisible Execution

The best compliment is no compliment at all — the work disappears behind the wall, and the house just works.

Pride, Reflected Back

Especially at the top of the ladder — a home that feels finished and flawless is part of how the owner experiences their own success.

Tyler's Internal Philosophy

"I want to do perfect work. I don't want my clients to worry about anything — so they can just look around their house and say, 'I'm rich!'"

This is the emotional target for every tier of the ICP, not just the luxury end. Whether the house is $400K or $3M, the job is the same: remove every worry until all that's left is a homeowner enjoying their own house.
