The Direction

Estate Green & Copper Wire.

Built on top of the existing black-and-white identity — a restrained, established palette that reads correctly whether it's on a $400K new build or a $3M estate.

Hunter green and a copper hairline — the color of the golf-course lots and manicured lawns across the service area, paired with a literal nod to the trade. Warm enough for family, disciplined enough for a master electrician.

How To Use It

Rules, not just colors.

01

Green carries the weight

Forest green is the dominant color — headers, hero bands, and the most important statement on any page. It should feel like the anchor, the way black used to.

02

Copper is a detail, never a fill

Used only for hairline underlines, left-edge accents on cards, small tags, and CTA buttons. If copper is covering more than a thin line or a button, it's being overused.

03

Parchment replaces white

No pure white and no pure black anywhere in the system — parchment and ink carry those roles instead, keeping everything warm.

Typography

A serif that reads as considered, not decorative.

Newsreader for anything that needs weight — headlines, the family story, testimonials. Inter carries every working sentence. A monospace utility face marks labels and data, like a spec sheet.

Signature Element

The copper hairline.

Section titles carry a short copper underline; cards and callouts carry a copper or forest-green left edge. This is the one recurring visual signature across every page — restrained, and never repeated as a full border or box.

Photography Direction

Real jobs, shot clean. Natural light, finished spaces, and the small details Tyler is proud of — not stock photography, not overly staged. The "invisible perfect work" philosophy means photography should show the result, not the process.

This is also where the Meta Ray-Ban POV social content becomes useful later — real job-site footage that still looks intentional against this palette, rather than raw phone-camera clutter.
